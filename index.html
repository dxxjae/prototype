About HIV

What is HIV?

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) is a virus that attacks the immune system, the body's natural defense against illness. If left untreated, HIV can lead to AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

How is HIV transmitted?

HIV is transmitted through blood, semen, vaginal fluids, and breast milk. The most common ways of transmission are through unprotected sex, sharing needles and syringes, and mother-to-child transmission during pregnancy, childbirth, or breastfeeding.

What are the symptoms of HIV?

Many people with HIV do not have any symptoms for years. However, some people may experience flu-like symptoms within 2 to 4 weeks after infection, such as fever, headache, muscle aches, and rash. The only way to know for sure if you have HIV is to get tested.

Prevention

Practice Safe Sex

Use condoms correctly every time you have vaginal, anal, or oral sex. Limit your number of sexual partners, and get tested for sexually transmitted infections (STIs) regularly.

Avoid Sharing Needles

Do not share needles or syringes for any reason. If you inject drugs, use clean needles and syringes every time, and never share works with others.

Get Tested Regularly

Know your HIV status by getting tested regularly. If you are living with HIV, take antiretroviral therapy (ART) as prescribed to suppress the virus and protect your health.

Treatment

Antiretroviral Therapy (ART)

ART is a combination of drugs that suppress the HIV virus and prevent its progression. With proper treatment, people living with HIV can lead long and healthy lives.

Pre-Exposure Prophylaxis (PrEP)

PrEP is a daily pill that can prevent HIV transmission. It is highly effective when taken as prescribed, but does not protect against other STIs.

Post-Exposure Prophylaxis (PEP)

PEP is a medication regimen that can prevent HIV transmission after exposure. It must be started within 72 hours of exposure to be effective.
